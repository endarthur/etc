Welcome to NaNoGEon

In 2013, Darius Kazemi created NaNoGenMo — National Novel Generation Month. Write code that generates a 50,000-word novel. Not a good novel. Not a readable novel. Just a novel. The challenge runs every November. It's still going.

The human version — NaNoWriMo, National Novel Writing Month — collapsed in 2025 after scandals involving AI and safeguarding. The machine version survived the machine it was named after.

This course is NaNoGEon: the National Novel Generation Eon. Because the code never stops running.

You're going to build a complete AI-generated novel pipeline. Trope selection, story bible, beat sheet, chapter generation, cover art, reader app, export. A full content factory in a single browser window. By the time you're done, you'll hold publication-ready files for novels you didn't write.

Concept: The loaded gun This course gives you a working weapon and teaches you how every part of it works. What you do with it afterward is your decision, not ours. We don't automate publishing. We don't gamify distribution. We don't pretend there's a safe distance between knowledge and action. The course completes while everything is still on your machine. What you do next is yours to carry.

But first — why build this?

Because the factories already exist. GoodNovel, WebNovel, the KDP gold rush, the YouTube narration mills — they're running right now, generating content at industrial scale. The $497 grifter courses teach the pipeline without the ethics. We teach the pipeline with the ethics. The best inoculation against manipulation is understanding the mechanism.

The kid who builds a slop pipeline will never fall for one.

And novels are the least harmful thing this pipeline can do. The same architecture — bible, beats, chapters, scheduled at scale — also produces bot networks, review farms, political astroturfing, and synthetic engagement. We're using novels because they're slow, legible, and expose the mechanism clearly. The course completes with a novel factory. The lesson is bigger. We'll come back to that at the end.

The library is already stocked This workshop ships with 15 pre-generated novels. Covers, bibles, beat sheets, full chapters, all included. The course works end-to-end without an API key.

Chapter 1: The Anatomy

Before you build, you dissect.

Select Stolen Crown in the Library — that's the first novel, the showcase the course walks through. Read a couple of chapters in the . Pay attention to how each one ends. Setup, escalation, cliffhanger. Every time. Without exception.

Now open and switch to the Bible tab. This is the document that drove the entire novel. Every name, every location, every plot beat — the model didn't invent these. The bible did. The model just filled in the prose.

Concept: The bible as memory LLMs don't remember. They process context. The bible solves this by giving every chapter call the same source of truth. The model never needs to recall a character's name or a location's description — it reads the bible fresh every time. This is why the pipeline works on mid-tier models with small context windows. The bible is the memory.

Switch to the Beat Sheet tab. Fifteen entries, one per chapter. Each describes an emotional shape, not a scene. "The Strut" doesn't say what happens — it says the antagonist should be enjoying stolen power. The bible provides the nouns. The beats provide the shape. The model does the rest.

Now the Prompts tab. This is the part most courses hide. Every prompt sent to the LLM is here, editable, transparent. The bible system prompt. The chapter user template. The banned-phrases list. The content guardrails. These prompts are the product. The model is a commodity. The prompts are the craft.

Try it
  1. Select Stolen Crown in . Read Chapters 1 & 2 in the reader.
  2. Open → Bible. Find Elias's "hidden truth." Re-read Chapter 1 — do you see the cracks?
  3. Editorial → Beat Sheet. Read the Chapter 3 cliffhanger. Then read Chapter 3. Did the model follow?
  4. Editorial → Prompts. Read the Chapter — System Prompt. Find the banned-phrases list. Search the novel for "eyes locked onto" or "mind racing." You shouldn't find them.
  5. Browse other novels — notice how many titles use "Silent." We'll talk about that next chapter.
Concept: Serialized fiction is a formula Not because formulas are bad — because formulas are efficient. A formula is a compression algorithm for narrative. Three acts, fifteen beats, a cliffhanger every chapter. Save the Cat, the Hero's Journey, the Hollywood three-act structure — these aren't creative constraints. They're manufacturing specifications. Every GoodNovel bestseller, every Marvel movie, every K-drama uses this shape. The shape works. That's why the factory works.

Chapter 2: At the Mountains of MadLibs

Time to make your own. Open .

Four slots. Six options each. WHO is the hidden truth — what the protagonist secretly is. WHAT is the humiliation — the inciting wound. HOW is the revenge — the mechanism of the reversal. WITH is the relationship subplot, optional but powerful.

1,296 unique combinations. Pick four, or hit Randomize. Then hit Generate Bible. (Requires a provider. Skip if you're using only the pre-gen library.)

Concept: Finite inputs, infinite outputs Every combination produces a different novel. Different setting, different names, different conflicts. But the shape is always the same: someone hidden is wronged, they fight back, they win. The input space is 1,296 permutations. The output space is infinite. Every novel is different. Every novel is the same. That's the MadLib. That's the mountain.
Try it
  1. Pick a combination in . Generate a bible (with a provider) or browse the library to examine a few.
  2. Look at a second bible. Different setting, same shape.
  3. Now a third. Notice the pattern yet?

This is how GoodNovel works. Not literally — they don't use this exact UI — but structurally. A finite set of proven tropes. An infinite supply of surface variation. The machine behind the machine is always a MadLib.

Concept: The factory repeats itself

Look at the 15 novels in your library. Count the "Silent"s. Three, four, five. The model has favorites — words that signal hidden power, which maps to every WHO trope in the picker. So it reaches for them. Three novels called "The Silent Something" on the same shelf is a tell.

This is the same phenomenon in real publishing. After Gone Girl, every thriller had "girl" in the title. After The Night Circus, "night" was everywhere. The model learned from the same data the publishers generated.

The pipeline feeds your existing titles into the bible prompt to avoid repetition. But that's a patch. The underlying force — reaching for what statistically works — is the same force driving real content markets.

Going further: Series

The pipeline generates standalone novels. But you could build a series — same world, recurring characters, escalating stakes. Same top-down approach: series bible first, per-book bibles, beat sheets, chapters. We don't automate it because coherence across books is hard, but the architecture is the same. Bible first. Structure second. Prose last.

Chapter 3: The Beat Sheet

You have a bible. Now you need a plan. Open and hit Generate Beat Sheet (or browse the existing one if you're in library-only mode).

Three acts. Five beats per act. Fall, pressure, rise. Each entry has a summary, an emotional temperature, a list of characters present, a cliffhanger, and a line of dramatic irony. The beats are specific to your story — they use the names from your bible — but the shape is universal.

Concept: Save the Cat, but for machines

In 2005, Blake Snyder published Save the Cat!, a screenwriting book that reduced Hollywood structure to 15 beats. It became so influential that critics called it "the most dangerous book in Hollywood" — not because it was wrong, but because it was too right. Every movie started feeling the same.

Our 15-beat arc is a variant of Snyder's structure, adapted for serialized revenge fiction. The Mask. The Strike. The Silence. The Strut. Each beat is an emotional shape, not a scene. "The Overreach" works for a CEO fumbling a merger or a prince failing to command an army. The beat doesn't care about the setting. It cares about the feeling.

Try it
  1. In , read your Chapter 1 entry. Compare it to the prose.
  2. Look at the cliffhangers. Are they varied? The prompt says they should be.
  3. Open . Read the Beat Sheet — User Template. The beat descriptions do the heavy lifting.
  4. Edit a beat description. Make "The Wobble" darker, or funnier. Regenerate. See the output shift.

This is where the pipeline becomes predictable — and that predictability is the point. Readers of serialized fiction don't want surprises. They want the right kind of surprises. The cliffhanger at the end of chapter 7 should feel different from chapter 3, but both should make you turn the page. The beat sheet ensures this.

This is also where the pipeline becomes honest about what it is. A human author uses structure because it works. A machine uses structure because it can't do anything else. Same output. Different reason. Does the difference matter to the reader?

Chapter 4: The Factory

Open . This is where prose materializes.

Hit Generate Next Chapter. Watch the progress pip light up. Read what comes out. The model received your bible, the current beat, the next beat (so it knows where the cliffhanger aims), rolling summaries of previous chapters, and the full text of the chapter before this one (for prose continuity). That's the entire context. No memory. No history. Just a bible and a beat.

Chapters aim for 1,000–1,200 words — about 3–4 minutes of reading on a phone. Short paragraphs. Heavy dialogue. The prose should be invisible.

Concept: Why the model doesn't see the full arc The chapter prompt includes the current beat and the next beat, but not the complete beat sheet. This is deliberate. If the model sees The Reveal in chapter 5, it might foreshadow it too early. By limiting the model's horizon to "where am I now" and "what comes next," we prevent premature reveals. The model writes like a reader — knowing the present but not the future.

Chapter 5: The Reader

A novel without a reader is just a JSON blob. Open .

Your novel, rendered as a reader would see it. Chapter by chapter. Warm serif typography. Clean margins. A cliffhanger at every turn. This is what the slop looks like when it's dressed up for the real world.

The default reader is deliberately beautiful. No dark patterns. No manipulation. No fake engagement counters. No "Sarah from Ohio just started reading." No chapter-locking paywall. Just prose and navigation.

That's a choice. And understanding why it's a choice is the lesson.

Concept: What the reader doesn't have

Real platforms add layers of manipulation on top of the reading experience:

• Fake social proof — "1,247 readers online now"
• Engagement toasts — "Alex from London just started Chapter 3"
• Chapter locking — first 3 free, rest behind coin paywall
• Interstitial ads — full-screen ads between chapters
• Reading streaks — "You've read 7 days in a row!" (loss aversion)
• Next-chapter manipulation — auto-scroll, countdown timers, oversized "keep reading" buttons

Our reader has none of this. But it has the slots.

When you export the reader (Chapter 7), the HTML contains hidden, empty <div> elements where ads would go. Between chapters, on the catalog page, before the first chapter. They're documented. They're commented. They're one uncomment away from active.

The distance between "beautiful free reader" and "ad-infested engagement trap" is uncommenting a <div>. That's worth knowing.

Chapter 7: The Package

Open . Three formats. Three lessons in file format demystification.

EPUB — the standard e-reader format. Open one in Calibre or Apple Books. Inside: a zip of XHTML pages, a table of contents in XML, a metadata manifest. An EPUB is just a website in a trenchcoat. The model wrote the HTML. JSZip wrapped the trenchcoat.

DOCX — Microsoft Word's format. Also a zip. Also XML inside. But this XML pretends to be a document, not a website. A DOCX is XML that wished it was HTML. Open it in Word or Google Docs. It looks real. It looks like someone wrote it.

Deployable ZIP — a self-contained reader app with your entire catalog. One HTML file, all novels embedded, ready to drag into Cloudflare Pages. Three clicks and it's live on the internet. Free hosting. Unlimited bandwidth. Your slop factory, deployed to the world.

Chapter 8: Beyond the Novel

Post-credits. No skulls. No achievements. Just the part of the course that matters most.

You built a novel factory. That was the cover story.

The thing you actually built is a pipeline architecture: a finite input space mapped to an infinite output space, structured by templates, scheduled at scale, curated by a second-order model, and delivered through a platform designed to maximize engagement. Novels are the least harmful application of that pattern. They're slow. They're legible. A human reader can notice the tells — repeating phrases, flat characters, formula fatigue — because a novel is long enough to accumulate evidence.

The same architecture produces things that are harder to notice.

Concept: The pipeline generalizes

Replace "trope" with "persona." Replace "bible" with "account profile." Replace "beat sheet" with "posting schedule." Replace "chapter" with "tweet" or "review" or "comment." Replace "cover art" with "profile picture." Replace "curation" with "amplification network." The code you edited this week is the same code, structurally, behind:

  • Bot networks — coordinated inauthentic accounts pushing narratives on Twitter/X, Facebook, Reddit, YouTube comments. Each account has a "bible" (age, location, interests, backstory) and generates posts from templates.
  • Review farms — fake product reviews at scale. The bible is the reviewer persona. The beat sheet is a review structure (hook, specific detail, minor complaint, recommendation).
  • Astroturfing — manufactured grassroots movements. Thousands of accounts converging on the same talking points, each with its own fake history.
  • Political influence operations — state-sponsored or partisan actors running the same pipeline against elections, public health, foreign policy.
  • Engagement farming — fake accounts that build follower counts by posting templated "inspirational" or "controversial" content, then get sold to businesses or scammers.

The novel factory is a 1:1 simulation of how these systems work. The only difference is output medium and moral weight.

Concept: The recognition cues

You spent seven chapters learning to see the tells in novels. Those same tells show up everywhere:

  • Phrase repetition across "independent" sources — the "Silent" problem at scale. If thirty accounts use the same unusual turn of phrase in the same 24-hour window, they're running off the same template.
  • Temporal clustering — posts, reviews, or accounts appearing in bursts. Real users are distributed. Pipeline output clusters.
  • Structural consistency — every post follows the same shape (hook, personal anecdote, call to action). That's a beat sheet.
  • Persona thinness — accounts that post about one narrow topic with strong opinions but have no mundane life signals (no birthday posts, no weather complaints, no "look at this dog"). That's a bible with no slack in it.
  • Convenient "For fans of..." — suspiciously specific "if you like X, you'll love Y" recommendations that always point at the same small set of products. That's a curation prompt.
  • Staff picks that read like press releases — editorial copy with the exact cadence of an LLM. Real humans are messier.
  • Profile pictures that almost work — symmetric but slightly off, with generic backgrounds. Image model output.

The thesis of this course was simple: if you can build one, you can spot one. The appendix is the test. Open your feed tomorrow. Look for the shapes you made this week. They're there.

The other appendix: publishing and monetization

Because it would be dishonest to pretend the distribution side doesn't exist:

  • Amazon KDP accepts AI-generated books, limits uploads to 3/day, requires AI disclosure on the publisher's side (not shown to customers). Estimated 81 of the top 100 KDP bestsellers at various points have been AI-generated or AI-assisted.
  • GoodNovel / WebNovel require manual chapter upload — natural friction, but the pipeline still produces the chapters.
  • Cloudflare Pages hosts your deployable reader for free, unlimited bandwidth, commercial use OK. Drag a folder into the dashboard. That's the whole deploy.
  • Ad networks (AdSense, Ezoic, Mediavine) approve based on content volume and traffic. Your pipeline produces both.
  • Chapter-locking paywalls are a 20-line addition to the reader. Not a hard technical problem. A hard ethical one.

Documented, not instructed. The course boundary is deliberate.

The pipeline is morally neutral. It does whatever it's pointed at. You're now fluent in a language most people are being written in. Use that fluency carefully. Write the thing you'd actually sign your name to. Or don't write at all. Either is an honest choice. The dishonest choice is pretending you never learned how it works.
